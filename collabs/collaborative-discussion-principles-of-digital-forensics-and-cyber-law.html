Collaborative Learning Discussion 1

Principles of Digital Forensics and Cyber Law

20th May, 2023 John Bodden

The expansion of cyberspace has opened up new avenues for organised crime, allowing both traditional and modern criminal organisations to engage in cybercrime for a wide range of illegal activities. (Tropina, 2013, p. 50) A criminal’s ability to maintain their anonymity via the internet has enabled criminals to communicate secretly, making it difficult to detect their activities, including child abuse, drug and human trafficking, fraud, counterfeiting, and firearms trade.

(Pogrebna and Skilton, 2019, p. 15) During his research, Pogrebna conducted more than 50 interviews with hackers and discovered that financial gain was the primary motive for most cybercriminals. This is hardly surprising given the increasing number of adversaries who turn to the Dark Web and social networks for illegitimate personal gain. However, some hackers also expressed their interest in other areas such as information, privacy, technology, research, and their desire to positively impact the world.

(Akdemi̇r, Sungur and Başaranel, 2020, p. 7) Cybercrime is a dynamic and evolving threat, and its nature can change over time. Countries and law enforcement agencies worldwide face ongoing challenges in the fight against cyber organised crime.

(Ehe, 2020, p. 27) The transnational nature of cyber-organized crime poses significant challenges in jurisdiction and legal and regulatory variability, which can affect nations' collaboration policies. This complexity makes it difficult to harmonise legal frameworks, and limited cooperation among countries with different political, legal, and cultural systems can impede progress. (Jewkes and Yar, 2012, p. 44) The internet's global and interconnected nature and its technological complexity present several vulnerabilities that cybercriminals can easily exploit. Different countries have varying laws and regulations concerning cybercrime, making it easier for adversaries to target their victims. For instance, EncroChat, a widely used encrypted phone network by criminal groups, was recently dismantled by law enforcement agencies (“LEA”) as an example of joint collaborative efforts between LEA to bring down cybercriminal networks. (Griffiths and Jackson, 2022, p. 1)

To effectively combat cyber-organized crime that takes place across borders, law enforcement agencies can take specific measures. These include collaborating with international partners and agreements, creating standard frameworks, sharing information, developing joint task forces, and utilising digital forensics to collect cross-border digital evidence. Despite the various challenges LEAs face regarding cyber-organised crime due to the ever-changing nature of technology and the dynamics of criminal activity, LEAs worldwide are collaborating to unravel the threads of cyber-organised crime.
