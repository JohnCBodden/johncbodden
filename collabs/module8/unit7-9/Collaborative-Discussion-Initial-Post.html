Collaborative Discussion 2: Units 7-9

Research Methods and Professional Practice

20th May, 2023 John Bodden

Abi's realisation that selectively analysing data could make Whizzz look better raises ethical, legal, social, and professional concerns. According to the general moral principles, “A computing professional should be honest and trustworthy. (Tractenberg, 2019).” If Abi were to present the data favouring Whizzz, even without changing it, he would be entering into an ethically questionable area known as 'data dredging' or 'p-hacking (Smith and Ebrahim, 2002)' This could lead to misleading conclusions and a significant erosion of public trust in scientific research. The potential harm is not just about misleading people; if the cereal is harmful, it could also impact public health. Abi must ensure the honesty and transparency of his analyses and include disclaimers about potential biases or study limitations to prevent misuse. However, Abi cannot control how the company uses the results.

Alternatively, Abi can create a detailed report that addresses this ethical dilemma. (Landis et al., 2012) The report should include the positive and negative findings and thoroughly explain the methods used and potential biases. Presenting only favourable results would mislead stakeholders and potentially harm public health if the cereal is harmful.

(Bird Stephanie J., 2014) It is best to advise that the company seek an independent third-party review of the data and analyses. This external review is crucial to ensuring the integrity and objectivity of the data analysis and maintaining the company's and Abi's professional reputation.

Abi can seek advice from an ethics committee or institutional review board to ensure his approach follows ethical guidelines.

If Abi suspects that the company might misuse the results to promote a potentially harmful product, he can consider reporting to regulatory authorities or public health organisations. From a legal, social, and professional perspective, providing misleading or incomplete information about a product’s safety could lead to severe legal issues for Abi and the company. Under laws like the Consumer Protection Act (Jacobs, Stoop and van Niekerk, 2010) in the UK or the Federal Trade Commission Act (Kovacic and Winerman, 2010) In the US, companies can face significant penalties for false advertising and not disclosing risks. This underscores the importance of adhering to ethical data analysis practices to avoid such legal pitfalls.

Disseminating misleading research can erode public trust in scientific institutions and professionals and directly affect public health if consumers are misinformed about the product's safety.

Abi’s reputation is at risk professionally. Engaging in unethical practices could damage his credibility and career prospects. Maintaining high ethical standards is crucial for long-term professional integrity and success.

Like many professionals in Statistical Practice, Abi is tempted to present data that only supports positive conclusions. While this may seem beneficial in the short term, it carries significant ethical, legal, social, and professional risks. Abi must prioritise transparency, objectivity, and the broader public good in this crucial moment. This means following established ethical guidelines and seeking support from ethical review bodies as needed.

Thank you, Francis; your post offers a comprehensive perspective on researchers' ethical responsibilities, particularly highlighting the importance of integrity, transparency, and honesty, as the Menlo Report emphasises (Finn & Shilton, 2023). It's crucial for all of us, as professional colleagues and peers, to adhere to these ethical guidelines in our research. This is paramount to ensure our findings are accurate, reliable, and free from conflicts of interest.

The consequences of misrepresenting research findings are severe and should not be underestimated. Such actions not only erode the integrity of the research but also have the potential to mislead stakeholders, leading to long-term adverse outcomes. (Scott, 2013) The British Computer Society's code of conduct rightly emphasises avoiding conflicts of interest, a point that Abi must carefully consider.

When relevant, presenting both positive and negative findings is not just a suggestion, but a crucial practice to avoid bias and maintain transparency. Even though Abi might not be obligated to present all analyses, doing so can provide a fuller picture and prevent misleading interpretations by stakeholders. This is a responsibility we all share as researchers.
